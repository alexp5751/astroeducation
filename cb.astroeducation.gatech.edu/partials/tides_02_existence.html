Why do tides exist?


The phenomenon of tides is explained by the force of gravity. Yes, the same gravity that causes apples to fall from trees and things that go up to come down, but on a different scale.

Gravity is one of the four fundamental forces: every object in the universe exhibits a gravitational pull on every other object. Sometimes we can see its effect during our daily lives, such as when someone drops a pencil on the ground. Other times, the force is much less noticeable, such as the pull between two atoms. Even if we can’t observe the gravitational pull between two objects visually, the same force equation applies to all objects. This includes the Earth and its moon.

Bigger objects, or objects with more mass, exert more gravitational force than smaller objects. The immense mass of the Earth is what keeps all the creatures, buildings, and air on this planet and allows life as we know it to exist. However, the sun is 1.3 million times as large as our Earth! We don’t fall into the sun since the sun is very far away, while Earth is directly under our feet. Similarly, the pull of the Earth’s gravity on an astronaut in space is much less than that of those on Earth. (Side note: An astronaut in orbit doesn't feel any gravity at all, because it is exactly countered by centrifugal force.) While larger objects generate stronger gravitational forces, the force of gravity falls off rapidly as distance increases.
